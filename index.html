HOW TO PLAY

THE IDEA

Piracy never died. You have a sloop, the clothes you stand in, and the actual planet — every coastline is the real one, scaled down, drawn by code. You start alone: a sloop sails single-handed, and hands are hired later, for crewing prizes. Money is made at sea: board merchantmen, take their ships whole, dig up what other pirates buried. The land holds treasure but earns nothing.

HANDLING HER

Walk the deck with WASD; press T to take the tiller from anywhere on deck (T again hands it back). At the tiller A/D steer and W/S sheet the mainsail in and out. This is real sailing: you cannot sail into the wind (“in irons” — the sail flaps, you stop), a beam reach (wind on your side) is fastest, and trim matters — the MAINSHEET bar glows green when your trim suits your course. A good sailor genuinely outruns a bad one. Leave the tiller and it is lashed to hold your course; she bears away on her own rather than stall.

GETTING AROUND THE PLANET

M opens the world chart, and the minimap is always up. In blue water a fair current carries you up to ×20 — so an Atlantic crossing takes minutes, not hours. The wind also builds as you leave the coast. Sighting another sail kills the current (“SAIL HO”) so ships can actually meet. The weather is real: the game fetches the live forecast at your ship’s true coordinates — a storm off the Azores today is a storm in the game, greying the sky and raising the swell.

PLUNDER

Merchantmen work the coasts — square sails, no flag. Run one down, pull alongside (within a couple of boat-lengths, speeds matched) and press E to board. Her purse is yours; about a third carry a treasure map, and sometimes a sailor signs on with you. Watch the horizon for the big slow Indiamen — treble the purse — and for the blue-hulled navy corvettes, which hunt pirates and shoot back.

THE GUNS

Your broadside fires perpendicular to the hull — there is no aiming, only seamanship: turn the ship until she bears, then F. Range is a musket-shot or so and closer is better. R swaps the load: round shot holes the hull and eventually sinks her (most cargo goes down; some floats — sail over the flotsam to hook it aboard); chain shot shreds sails and slows a runner so you can board her whole. Reloading is slow alone and quick with a crew. Damage works both ways: a corvette's iron tears your rig and hull — watch the panel. The yard at any port repairs her for coin. Boarding an armed ship is a real fight — the crews decide it by weight of numbers, so count your hands before you leap. And when you are out-gunned (in a sloop, you are): run. A well-trimmed sloop outruns any corvette afloat, and shallow water ends every chase — a warship's keel dare not follow where yours can go.

FOUNDERING & WRECKING — WHAT SINKING COSTS

Nobody dies in Saltstead, but the sea keeps accounts, in two doses. Holed through once, the crew heaves a third of your gold overboard to keep her afloat and she limps on CRIPPLED (the damage panel says so) until a yard mends her. Holed through again while crippled, she sinks. Everyone lives — the longboat lands the crew, your treasure map, the ship’s log and a tenth of the chest at the nearest port — but the ship, the prizes astern and the rest of the gold belong to the sea. A wreck also drops you a rung down the shipwright's ladder (a sunk brig hands you a schooner; a sunk sloop captain is staked a patched sloop by the harbour), so the voyage always goes on. Two habits keep it cheap: repair the moment she’s hurt, and bank what you can’t bear to lose in Davy Jones’ Locker — banked gold is beyond the sea’s reach, wreck or no wreck. (Refreshing the page won’t save you: her hurts ride the save.)

PRIZES AND YOUR FLEET

After boarding, stay alongside and press E again to put a prize crew aboard (3 hands — your sloop sails fine without them). She falls in astern and sails with you, up to three prizes in column. Prizes are worth far more than their purses — if you have the hands, take the ship.

PORTS — A WORLD OF THEM

Two kinds of harbour work for you. Four pirate havens fly no colours — Port Royal, Nassau, Tortuga, and Île Sainte-Marie off Madagascar — and they fence prizes at full price, no questions asked. Beyond them, dockyards ring the whole world: Havana, Lisbon, Cape Town, Bombay, Canton, Acapulco, Rio and more (small anchors on the chart). Every one of them repairs, signs on hands, and keeps a shipwright — so a voyage never has to beat back to the Caribbean for a new topmast. Honest ports ask questions, though: a prize fetches only half there.

Arriving is simple: run her right up the beach — the sloop draws so little the bow takes the sand and she stops — and press E to put in. (Bigger hulls draw too much: they fetch up on the shoal offshore and E sends the longboat in — the game tells you when.) Gold buys crew, crew takes prizes: that is the game.

THE SHIPWRIGHT'S LADDER

You start in a sloop: one gun a side, twelve berths, the fastest and shallowest thing afloat. She is not a warship — she is a predator of traders and a keeper of secrets. Survive, get rich, and any port's shipwright will build you up a seven-rung ladder: sloop → cutter → schooner → brig → corvette → frigate → galleon. Every rung buys guns, berths and speed and spends handiness and shallow water — the schooner is the last hull that beaches, and the galleon trades the frigate’s legs for six guns a side and a sterncastle. Every hull changes how you survive, and the Captain's Briefing (button below, and it pops whenever you take a new hull to sea) tells you exactly how to stay alive in yours.

COLLISION & RAMMING

Hulls are solid. Touch another ship gently and the hulls shoulder apart with curses; drive your bow in at speed and both ships take hull damage — the lighter one takes more. A galleon can stove in a schooner’s side; a sloop that rams a corvette mostly hurts herself. The navy knows it too: a hunting corvette holds off and circles at gun range rather than fouling her own bow.

TREASURE

A looted map inks an X on your charts. Sail within reach of the X and press E to row in and dig. Or make landfall yourself: beach the boat on any coast and E puts you ashore. Walk to the X on foot and drive the spade in with your own hands. A chest pays several prizes’ worth. To leave a beach, take the tiller and steer her back off the sand.

THE NAVIGATOR’S CRAFT

L opens the ship’s log — the game keeps it for you, every event stamped with the watch (Middle, Morning, Forenoon…) and your position in degrees and minutes, the way a master wrote it. N opens the star chart, drawn from the same sky over your deck. On a clear night the navigator takes a sight: the Pole Star’s height above the horizon IS your latitude — the fact sailors steered by for a thousand years. Sail south and watch Polaris sink; cross the line and the Southern Cross takes over. The wildlife navigates too: gulls mean land is near, the albatross means true blue water, and a fin in warm shallows means don’t swim.

THE LEGENDS

Every mark on the world chart is real geography with a story on it, and the stories are live. In the Bermuda Triangle the fog closes in, the instruments lie — position, chart, heading, all of it — and dead ships drift full of cargo; navigate out by the sun. The Corryvreckan whirlpool off Scotland slings you round its rim, but the core will shred your rigging. The Kraken waits in the Norwegian deeps: axes and broadsides cut it loose arm by arm, or run for shallow water where it cannot follow. Dragons nest on Snowdonia — she only comes into gunshot when she stoops on your rig; wound her thrice and follow her ashore to her hoard. The Flying Dutchman rounds the Cape of Good Hope in every storm — iron passes through her, but a bold crew can board her mid-tempest. Dive the 1715 Plate Fleet off Florida for Spanish silver. Sail up the Amazon to find El Dorado. And over the deepest water on earth waits Davy Jones’ Locker: gold consigned there is banked forever.
